Introduction

What Is
Settlers: Capitalism?

A love letter to chaos, debt, and the beauty of ruined friendships.

Settlers: Capitalism is a fan-made expansion layer built on top of the classic board game Catan (formerly known as Settlers of Catan). You still need a physical Catan board, pieces, and dice. What this system adds is an entire living economic engine — powered by software — that runs alongside the base game.

Where standard Catan ends at trading and building, Settlers: Capitalism introduces a full financial ecosystem: savings accounts, variable-rate loans, a live stock market, rental income, global shipping orders, progressive taxation, and spontaneous economic disasters that nobody voted for.

How to Read This Rulebook

Chapters 01 covers the base Catan mechanics — read this if you're new. Chapters 02–11 are the Capitalism rules layered on top. Each chapter builds on the last. The software handles all the math automatically; this rulebook explains the why behind the systems.

What You Need

  • A physical Catan board (base game, any edition)
  • One shared device running the Settlers: Capitalism software
  • 2–4 players (or more with expansion boards)
  • A Bank Manager — one player designated to perform the ritual if required
  • A willingness to have your savings wiped at any moment

The Philosophy

Standard Catan is a beautiful game of resource management and negotiation. Settlers: Capitalism takes that foundation and asks: what if the board game also had a housing market, a stock exchange, and the occasional depression?

The answer, it turns out, is that the game becomes significantly more chaotic, significantly more political, and significantly more fun. Every economic decision has cascading consequences. A well-timed loan can secure a City that wins you the game. A late repayment can block you from victory for three agonizing turns. A Bank Collapse can erase everyone's savings in an instant.

Friendship Warning

This game has been known to damage relationships. The taxation system is particularly contentious. Proceed with people you trust — or people you're willing to lose.

The Software

The Settlers: Capitalism software is a digital banker assistant that automatically tracks:

  • Each player's hand resources vs. saved (banked) resources
  • All active loans, interest accrual, and overdue penalties
  • Stock prices — updated dynamically each turn
  • Economic events (Boom, Recession, Depression, Crisis, Bank Collapse)
  • Rentals, shipping orders, and debit/credit card maintenance
  • Victory Points and win eligibility

The software is the single source of truth. If there's a dispute, the software is correct. Except for the Bank Collapse ritual. That requires human intervention.

Base Catan
How the Board Works

If you already know Catan, skim this. If you don't, read every word.

Settlers: Capitalism is built on top of the standard Catan ruleset. This chapter covers the base mechanics. Everything in Chapters 02–11 is layered on top of this foundation.

The Resources

There are five resource types in Catan. Each hex on the board produces one type. When the matching number is rolled, everyone with a settlement or city adjacent to that hex receives that resource.

🧱 BRICK Hills

⛏️ ORE Mountains
Used for Cities & Dev Cards. Boosts on 9, 12.
🌾 GRAIN Fields

Your Turn (Base Game)

1
Roll Both Dice
The sum determines which hexes produce. All players with settlements or cities adjacent to matching hexes receive resources. Settlements yield 1, Cities yield 2.
2
Trade
Negotiate with other players (domestic trade). Only the active player can initiate trades — others cannot trade amongst themselves. You may also trade with the bank at 4:1, or better rates at harbors (3:1 generic, 2:1 specific).
3
Build
Spend resources to place roads, settlements, cities, or buy development cards. You can build as many things as you can afford in one turn.

Rolling a 7

When a 7 is rolled, no resources are produced. Instead:

  1. Every player with more than 7 cards must discard half (round down).
  2. The active player moves the Robber to any hex, blocking its production until moved again.
  3. The active player steals 1 random card from a player with a settlement or city adjacent to the Robber's new hex.
Capitalism Modifier

In Settlers: Capitalism, banked (saved) resources are NOT affected by the Robber. Only resources in your hand can be stolen or discarded on a 7. This makes saving strategically valuable — and also means the Robber is slightly less terrifying than in base Catan.

Development Cards

TypeEffect
KnightMove the Robber. Steal from an adjacent player. Collect 3 to claim Largest Army (+2 VP).
Road BuildingPlace 2 free roads immediately.
Year of PlentyTake any 2 resources from the supply.
MonopolyName a resource type. All other players give you every card of that type.
Victory PointHidden until you declare victory. Worth 1 VP each.
Key Rule

You can only play 1 development card per turn. You cannot play a card bought on the same turn (except Victory Point cards when winning).

Harbors

Settlements or cities built on harbor intersections unlock better maritime trade rates:

  • Generic Harbor (3:1): Trade 3 of any same resource for 1 of anything.
  • Specific Harbor (2:1): Trade 2 of the shown resource for 1 of anything.
  • No Harbor: Always 4:1 with the bank.

Longest Road & Largest Army

Longest Road: The first player to build a continuous road of at least 5 segments claims the Longest Road card (+2 VP). If another player builds longer, they take it immediately — a 4 VP swing.

Largest Army: The first player to play 3 Knight cards claims this card (+2 VP). If another player plays more knights, they take it.

Strategic Note

You can break an opponent's Longest Road by building a settlement on an intersection along their road. This can cause a massive VP swing even mid-game.

Core Rules

Winning
the Game

10 Victory Points on your own turn. Simple. Except it isn't.

The goal of Settlers: Capitalism is identical to base Catan: be the first player to reach 10 Victory Points on your own turn. However, Capitalism introduces several Win Blockers that can prevent you from declaring victory even when you have the points.

Victory Point Sources

SourceVP ValueNotes
🏠 Settlement1 VPBuilt on board intersections
🏙️ City2 VPUpgrades a settlement (does not stack — replaces it)
🛣️ Longest Road+2 VPMust have 5+ continuous roads AND be the longest
⚔️ Largest Army+2 VPMust have 3+ knights AND be the largest
🃏 VP Dev Card+1 VP eachHidden until declaring victory
Quick Math

You start with 2 settlements (2 VP). To win, you need 8 more. Maximum from buildings alone: 5 settlements (5 VP) + 4 cities (8 VP) = 13 VP. But you only get 5 settlements and 4 cities total, and cities replace settlements. In practice: 1 settlement + 4 cities = 1 + 8 = 9 VP from buildings. Then you need 1 more from roads, army, or dev cards.

Win Blockers

Even if you have 10+ VP, you cannot declare victory while any of these conditions are active:

Win Blocker #1 — Overdue Loan

Any overdue loan — standard or credit — blocks victory. You must repay the full amount (including penalties) before you can win.

Win Blocker #2 — Post-Repayment Wait

After paying off an overdue loan, you must wait 3 full turns before you can declare victory. This prevents players from strategically delaying repayment until they're ready to win.

Win Blocker #3 — Active Recession or Depression

Victory is suspended during active Recession (4 turns) and throughout all phases of a Depression (7 turns). No one can win while the economy is in freefall.

Declaring Victory

At the start of your turn, if you have 10+ VP and no active blockers, you may declare victory. Reveal any hidden Victory Point dev cards at this moment. The game ends immediately.

Timing Rule

You cannot win on another player's turn — even if you would have enough VP. Wait for your turn, then declare. If someone else reaches 10 VP between your turns, they win before you get the chance.

03
Core Rules

Turn
Structure

Every turn, in five clean steps. The software handles steps 2 and 4.

Each turn in Settlers: Capitalism follows a fixed five-step structure. Steps 2 and 4 are resolved automatically by the software. Steps 1, 3, and 5 require player action.

STEP 1
Roll Dice
Roll both dice. Resources are produced for all settlements and cities on matching hexes. Rolling the exact same total as the previous turn triggers a Bank Collapse. The software tracks the previous roll automatically.
STEP 2 — AUTO
Resource Distribution
The software automatically updates every player's hand and banked resources. Banked resources are not affected by the Robber on a 7. Only hand resources are at risk of theft, event penalties, or discarding.
STEP 3 — ACTIONS
Optional Actions (Any Order)
In any order, the active player may take any or none of the following actions:
  • Build — roads, settlements, cities, dev cards
  • Trade — with other players or the bank/harbors
  • Bank — deposit/withdraw savings, take loans, use cards
  • Shipping — place resource orders for future delivery
  • Rentals — set or collect rental arrangements
  • Gamble — use the gambling feature at your peril
  • Stocks — buy or sell resource shares
STEP 4 — AUTO
Event Resolution
The software automatically checks for overdue loans, approaching maintenance payments, active economic events, and Bank Collapse triggers. Anything flagged is resolved before the turn ends.
STEP 5
Turn Summary
All players review VP totals, hand resources, banked amounts, active loans, and rankings. If the active player has 10+ VP and no blockers, they may declare victory. Otherwise, pass the device to the next player.
Strategy: Order of Operations

Since Step 3 actions can be taken in any order, consider your sequencing carefully. For example: withdraw savings → buy stocks → trade → build vs. build first → then trade for what you're missing. The order matters, especially when loans, maintenance, and events interact.

Card Perks

Debit and Credit cards modify building costs in different ways:

CardBuilding PerkCatch
Debit Card Ignore 1 resource cost when building (you choose which) Costs 2 resources to open + 1 maintenance every 3 turns
Credit Card Automatically covers 1 missing resource when building Creates a Credit Loan — due in 2 turns with 10% interest
Credit Card Warning

The Credit Card's building perk sounds amazing. It is. But every time it activates, it creates a Credit Loan. Credit Loans are due in just 2 turns. Miss that window and you're paying +20% per late turn — and you're blocked from winning, trading, and building. Use the credit card strategically, not habitually.

Boom Modifier

During an active Boom event (3 turns), all build costs are reduced by 1 resource, with a minimum of 1 resource per build. This is the best time to spam settlements if you've been saving resources.

The Distance Rule

A settlement can only be placed at an intersection if all 3 adjacent intersections are empty — no settlements or cities of any player can be within 1 road segment. This applies at all times. Plan your road network with future settlement sites in mind.

Pro Tip

Build roads to "claim" settlement spots before you can afford the settlement. Your opponents can't build there as long as you have a road leading to the adjacent intersection — as long as no other settlement violates the Distance Rule.

Rankings
& Taxation

The rich get taxed. The poor get a break. The daring get caught.

Every player has a Rank determined by their total asset value. Rankings affect how much tax you pay every 5 global turns — and unlock the option of tax evasion for the bold.

How Rankings Work

Rankings are calculated from assets only. Hand resources do NOT count.

Note on Rankings

Rankings are recalculated by the software each turn. You can move between tiers as your savings fluctuate, your properties are built or lost, and stock prices shift. A Bank Collapse can dramatically reshuffle rankings by wiping savings and crashing stocks.

Taxation

Every 5 global turns, a tax event fires automatically. Tax is charged per property unit you own.

RankTax RateExample: 4 property units
Rich2 resources per property unit8 resources
Middle1 resource per property unit4 resources
PoorExempt0 resources

Payment Order

When tax is due, you pay from your resources in this order:

  1. Hand resources — pay directly from your hand
  2. Sell resources — if hand is insufficient, sell to generate resources
  3. Sell property — forced sales at half price if still insufficient
Forced Property Sale

Being forced to sell property at half value is extremely punishing. Keep enough liquid resources to cover your expected tax bill, especially if you're in the Rich tier with significant property holdings.

Tax Evasion

Once per tax cycle, any player can attempt to evade taxes. Roll 1 die:

RollResult
1 – 3Caught. Pay double tax.
4 – 6Escaped. Pay nothing this cycle.
When to Evade

Tax evasion is a 50/50. It's highest-value when you're Rich with a large property portfolio — the double-tax penalty hurts, but the potential savings are enormous. If you're Middle tier with just a few properties, the risk/reward is less compelling.

Economy

Banking
& Loans

Safe storage. Dangerous debt. The difference matters.

The banking system is the economic spine of Settlers: Capitalism. It separates your resources into two categories: hand resources (fast, flexible, risky) and saved resources (slow, safe, productive). Loans let you borrow against the future — at a cost.

Hand vs. Saved Resources

Robber (7)Can be stolen. Discard if 8+.Safe. Never affected by 7.
EventsAt risk from Bank Collapse, etc.Wiped by Bank Collapse only.
BuildingUse directlyCannot use directly — withdraw first
GrowthNone+1 per 3 banked, every 3 turns
RankingNot countedCounts toward asset ranking

Savings Account

  • Cost to open: 2 resources (one-time)
  • Growth: Every 3 turns, earn +1 resource for every 3 resources you have banked
  • 7-roll bonus: When a 7 is rolled, earn +1 resource per resource type currently in savings
  • Safe from: The Robber, hand-discard rules, most events
  • Not safe from: Bank Collapse (wipes savings entirely)
Savings Strategy

The 7-roll bonus rewards diversified savings. Storing 1 of each resource type (5 resources total) yields +5 bonus on any 7 roll — significantly more than the base growth rate. Diversify your savings if you can afford the range.

Debit Card

  • Cost: 2 resources to issue
  • Maintenance: 1 resource every 3 turns
  • Perks: Direct withdrawal from savings (no manual step needed) + 1 resource discount on any build action

Credit Card

  • Cost: 3 resources to issue
  • Maintenance: 1 resource every 5 turns
  • Perk: Automatically covers 1 missing resource when building → creates a Credit Loan

The Debt Spiral

A standard loan of 10 resources, missed by 3 turns, with 2 previous late marks, costs 10 × 1.30 × 1.25³ = ~25 resources. That's more than double. And you can't trade, build, or win while it's outstanding. The software enforces this. Plan repayment before you borrow.

The Stock
Market

Buy low. Sell high. Panic when the dice roll badly.

Every resource type has a tradeable stock. You buy shares using resources and sell them back for resources. Prices fluctuate every turn based on the state of the game — creating opportunities for passive income and speculative plays.

Core Rule

You cannot buy stocks while any loan is overdue. You can always sell, regardless of loan status.

Strategy

Before a Boom

If you anticipate a Boom event (common after a Depression), load up on stocks. Boom adds +0.25 per turn for 3 turns — that's a potential +0.75 gain per share on top of any baseline appreciation.

Before a Recession

Sell before a Recession if you can see it coming (after a Bank Collapse with few sellers). Recession drives prices down −0.20/turn for 4 turns.

Dice Reading

If a 5 is frequently being rolled, Wool stocks are being boosted. Buy Wool before the next 5. This sounds simple but requires you to track dice history — which the software shows in the game log.

Passive Income Play

Stocks don't have to be day-traded. Holding a diversified portfolio across all 5 resource types during stable economic periods generates consistent returns as natural scarcity fluctuations play out. It's less exciting but reliably profitable.

08
Economy

Shipping
& Rentals

Passive income systems. Both get canceled in a downturn. Plan accordingly.

Global Shipping

Shipping lets you order any resource from the global market, delivered in a future turn. It's useful for securing resources you can't easily produce from your hex placement.

Core Rule

You cannot pay the shipping fee with the same resource type you're ordering. If you're ordering Ore, the fee must be paid in any other resource(s).

Shipping Fees

Order SizeStandard FeeExpedited Fee
1–2 units+1 resource+2 resources
3–6 units+3 resources+6 resources
7+ units+4 resources+8 resources

Delivery Speed

  • Standard: Arrives in 3 turns from order date
  • Expedited: Arrives next turn — fee is doubled
Recession / Depression Warning

All shipping is suspended during Recession and Depression. Orders currently in transit are canceled with no refund. Do not place large orders if an economic downturn seems likely. The software will warn you if conditions are deteriorating.

Shipping Strategy

Shipping is best used to fill specific resource gaps — for example, ordering 3 Ore to complete a City when your board position doesn't produce mountains well. The 3-turn delay means you need to plan ahead. Expedited is almost never worth double the fee unless you're desperate for a win-threatening build.

Rentals

You can rent any of your properties — settlements, cities, or roads — to other players in exchange for resources. It's the Catan version of passive income.

Rental Rules

  • The landlord retains full VP ownership. Renting does not transfer points.
  • Settlements & Cities: Tenant pays each turn the rental is active.
  • Roads: Tenant pays every 3 turns.
  • Rental rates are negotiated between players — the software tracks and enforces the agreed terms.
  • Rentals are suspended during Recession and Depression. No payments occur during those periods.
Landlord Strategy

Renting a settlement in a high-production hex is lucrative. You keep the VP and receive passive resources every turn. The tenant benefits because they gain access to production from hexes they don't control. Both sides can win from a well-structured rental — just make sure you're getting the better deal.

Downturn Risk

If you're relying on rental income to repay a loan, a sudden Recession can cut off that income stream while the loan keeps accruing penalties. Always have a fallback repayment plan that doesn't depend on rentals continuing.

The Depression — Three Phases

A Depression is the most severe economic event in the game. It runs for 7 global turns divided into three distinct phases, each with different rules.

PHASE 1
Immediate Shock
TURNS 1–2
• All players lose 50% hand resources (round up)
• All stocks immediately −50%
• All loans become immediately due
• Shipping and rentals frozen
PHASE 2
Market Stagnation
TURNS 3–5
• No new loans allowed
• No savings usage
• No stock purchases
• Trading between players allowed
• Building allowed
PHASE 3
Recovery
TURNS 6–7
• Savings access restored
• Credit cards allowed
• Shipping resumes
• VP collection still blocked until Depression ends
After the Depression

Once all 7 turns complete, victory becomes possible again. A Boom may automatically trigger after the Depression — rewarding players who survived with discounted building costs and rising stocks. Position yourself for the Boom during Phase 3.

Bank Collapse → Recession or Depression?

When a Bank Collapse occurs, the number of players selling stocks determines what follows:

Sellers During CollapseEvent Triggered
0–1 sellersRecession — 4 turns
2+ sellersDepression — 7 turns

This creates a fascinating collective action problem: selling saves your stock value in the short term but triggers a deeper crisis for everyone. Not selling is a form of cooperation — even if nobody asked for your cooperation.

10
Events

The Bank
Collapse

The most chaotic single event in the game. And someone has to hit the floor.

The Bank Collapse is the nuclear option of Settlers: Capitalism. It fires when a player rolls the exact same dice total as the previous turn. The software tracks previous rolls and triggers the collapse automatically.

Trigger

Trigger Condition

Roll the same total as the previous turn's roll. Example: if the last roll was 8 (5+3), and you roll 8 (4+4) this turn, the Bank Collapse fires. The probability of this happening on any given turn is approximately 1 in 6.

Immediate Effects

1
Lose 50% Hand Resources
Every player loses half their hand resources, rounded up. Richer players (those with more in hand) lose more in absolute terms. Savings are wiped completely — see Step 3.
2
Stocks Drop 50%
All stock prices immediately halve. If you hold shares, their value is cut in half instantly. This is the fastest way to crater a portfolio in the game.
3
All Loans Immediately Due
Every outstanding loan — regardless of when it was taken — becomes immediately due. If you can't pay, you're instantly overdue, with all associated penalties and blocks.
4
Savings Wiped
All banked (saved) resources are completely erased. Every player's savings account goes to zero. Years of careful saving, gone in one roll.
5
Shipping & Rentals Canceled
All in-transit shipping orders are canceled with no refund. All active rental agreements are suspended.

What Follows

After the Bank Collapse effects resolve, the game tracks how many players sell stocks in response:

  • 0–1 sellers: Recession begins (4 global turns)
  • 2+ sellers: Depression begins (7 global turns, 3 phases)

The Ritual

This is non-negotiable. When a Bank Collapse fires, the designated Bank Manager must perform the following ritual. The honor system is the only enforcement mechanism. But the honor system is real.

⚠ BANK COLLAPSE ⚠
The financial system has imploded. The Bank Manager must now:
🫳 Fall onto the floor. Physically. On the ground. Now.
😭 Cry dramatically. Wailing encouraged. Tears optional.
📢 Lament the downfall of the entire financial system.
⚠️ Failure to perform = game ends with NO WINNER
Strategic Note

You can partially hedge against a Bank Collapse by keeping minimal savings (so there's less to wipe), keeping loans repaid (so there's nothing to become immediately due), and holding some resources in hand rather than stocks (though hand resources are also halved). There's no perfect hedge — only degrees of suffering.
